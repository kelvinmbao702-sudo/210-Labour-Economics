01
Introduction to Labour Economics
The study of how labour markets work — who gets hired, at what wage, and why.
Core Definition

Labour economics applies economic analysis to labour markets. It studies decisions of workers (maximize well-being) and employers (maximize profit), and the public policies affecting employment and wages.

📊
Socioeconomic Insight

Explains unemployment, minimum wages, wage inequality, and workplace safety issues.

📈
Trend Analysis

Explains rise of service sector, female labour force participation, decline in unions, globalisation.

⚙️
Resource Allocation

Labour = most important productive factor. Labour economics guides efficient allocation of human resources.

🏛️
Policy Formulation

Informs government policies on employment standards, human rights, labour relations, and social security.

Key Characteristics of Labour Economics
Derived Demand

Labour demand comes from demand for the final goods labour helps produce.

Institutional Context

Heavily shaped by laws, unions, social norms, and institutions.

Complex Price (Wage)

Wage serves 3 roles: allocative, incentive, and income-distribution.

Micro + Macro

Individual decisions (micro) AND aggregate employment/inflation (macro).

Real-World Examples

📌 Min wage increase → predict job losses (demand) vs income gains (supply)
📌 Firm automating → compares labour cost vs capital cost using derived demand & substitution effects

02
The Labour Market
The arena where labour services are bought (employers) and sold (workers).
🏢
Internal Market

Rules within one firm governing hiring, promotion & pay. E.g., seniority-based ladders.

📍
Local Market

Search within a limited geographic area. E.g., restaurant servers in a city.

🌐
National Market

Search occurs nationwide. E.g., software engineers, university professors.

ActorPrimary Objective
👷 WorkersMaximise utility — best combo of wages, benefits & working conditions
🏭 FirmsMaximise profits — hire optimal workers at lowest total compensation cost
🏛️ GovernmentSet legal/regulatory environment — balance worker protection with employer competitiveness
Why Labour Markets ≠ Regular Product Markets
Labour is Rented

Workers can't be separated from their services — non-wage factors are critically important.

Non-Pecuniary Factors

Environment, safety risk, manager personality, and perceived fairness all matter.

Legal Constraints

Min wage laws, health & safety regs, human rights legislation, union contracts.

Imperfect Info

High search costs for both workers finding best jobs and firms finding best workers.

Key Examples

📌 Same job with/without A/C → different supply curves → compensating wage differential
📌 Remote mining town with ONE employer → Monopsony → wages set below competitive level

03
The Demand for Labour
How many workers a firm wants to hire at each possible wage — driven by what those workers produce.
Derived Demand — The Core Idea

Firms don't hire workers for fun. They hire because workers produce goods & services that consumers want. If car demand drops → auto worker demand drops. Labour demand is derived from product demand.

⚡ Short Run

  • Capital is fixed — only labour varies
  • Hire until MRP = Wage
  • MRP curve = firm's demand curve
  • Slopes DOWN (diminishing returns)
  • Less elastic response to wage changes

🔄 Long Run

  • All inputs (capital + labour) are variable
  • Firms can substitute capital for labour
  • Demand curve is more elastic (flatter)
  • Greater adjustment to wage changes
  • E.g., install kiosks if min wage rises
What shifts Labour Demand?

Productivity ↑

Better training or tech → each worker produces more → demand shifts RIGHT

Cheaper Capital

Ambiguous: scale effect (↑ labour) vs. substitution effect (↓ labour)

More Employers

More firms in market → total labour demand increases

04
The Supply of Labour
How individuals decide how many hours to work — the tension between earning more and enjoying leisure.
Work-Leisure Model

Each person has 24 hours. They split it between work (earn income) and leisure (everything unpaid). Higher wages change this trade-off in two competing ways.

💱 Substitution Effect

  • Wage ↑ → leisure becomes more expensive
  • Opportunity cost of not working rises
  • Individual substitutes work for leisure
  • → Works MORE hours
  • Dominates at LOW wages

💰 Income Effect

  • Wage ↑ → same income needs fewer hours
  • Leisure is a "normal good" → buy more of it
  • Individual purchases more leisure
  • → Works FEWER hours
  • Dominates at HIGH wages
The Backward-Bending Supply Curve

At high enough wages, people choose to work less — they can "afford" more leisure.

Examples

📌 Student gets raise $10→$15/hr → takes MORE shifts (substitution dominates)
📌 Surgeon gets another raise → takes Fridays off (income effect dominates — she can afford it)

What shifts Labour Supply?
Non-Wage Income ↑

Inheritance/lottery → supply shifts LEFT (work less)

Better Job Conditions

Flex hours, good environment → supply shifts RIGHT

Preferences Change

Cultural shifts, family duties → shift in indifference map

05
Wage Determination & Equilibrium
How supply and demand interact to set the market wage — and how that changes under different market structures.
3 Functions of Wages

🔀 Allocative — guides workers to where their labour is most valued
⚡ Incentive — encourages education, training, and work effort
💵 Income Distribution — determines workers' standard of living

Feature🟢 Perfect Competition🔴 Monopsony
Firm's Labour Supply Perfectly elastic (horizontal line at market wage) Upward-sloping — must raise wage for ALL to hire one more
Marginal Wage Cost (MWC) MWC = Wage MWC lies ABOVE the supply curve
Hiring Rule MRP = W MRP = MWC
Outcome Higher wage (Wc), Higher employment (Qc) Lower wage (Wm), Lower employment (Qm)

🟢 Perfect Competition Example

  • Many baristas in a large city
  • New café MUST pay going rate ($16/hr)
  • No single firm influences wage
  • Everyone is a wage-taker

🔴 Monopsony Example

  • One large factory in a small rural town
  • To hire more → must raise wage for ALL
  • High marginal labour cost → hires fewer
  • Pays below competitive wage

The Principal-Agent Problem

Firm (principal) wants: maximum profit
Worker (agent) wants: maximum utility (least effort for most pay)
Workers may shirk — provide less effort than agreed. Firms use pay structures to align these interests.

🍎

Pay ABOVE market-clearing level voluntarily

✓ Reduces shirking & turnover

07
Wage Differentials
Why identical workers earn different wages across jobs, locations, and industries.

Equilibrium Differentials

  • Persistent — they don't cause workers to move
  • E.g., dangerous jobs always pay more
  • Workers are compensated for undesirable features

Transitional Differentials

  • Temporary — create mobility incentives
  • Workers move to close the gap over time
  • Eventually arbitraged away by market forces
Hedonic Theory of Wages

Workers and firms match based on preferences. Risk-averse workers take safe jobs with lower wages. Risk-tolerant workers take dangerous jobs with compensating wage premiums. The wage and working conditions are jointly determined.

Compensating
Extra pay for undesirable job features
🦀 Alaskan crab fisherman: extreme risk = very high wage
Human Capital
Higher pay for greater skills & education
🧠 Neurosurgeon earns far more than LPN due to training investment
Efficiency Wage
Voluntary above-market wage
🏭 Large plant pays more to cut costly turnover and shirking
Union Status
Unionized workers typically earn more
⚡ Unionized electrician earns more + better benefits vs non-union
Geographic
Differences in cost of living & amenities
🏙️ SF software engineer earns more to offset higher housing costs
Imperfections
Barriers: licensing, discrimination
🦷 Dentist licensing restricts supply → wages above competitive level

📘 Tutorial 1 — Foundations
Q1What is labor economics? Which types of questions do labor economists analyze?
Labour economics studies the labour market, focusing on the efficient use of the economy's scarce labour resources.
  • What determines wages?
  • What causes unemployment?
  • What is the impact of immigration on native-born workers?
  • What is the effect of a minimum wage on employment?
Q2Who are the key actors in the labor market? What motives do economists assign to workers and firms?
Key actors: Workers, Firms, Government
  • Workers → maximize utility (well-being)
  • Firms → maximize profits
  • Government → set legal & regulatory framework
Q3What is the difference between positive and normative economics? Why are positive questions easier to answer?
  • Positive economics → objective, testable "what IS" statements. Can be resolved by data.
  • Normative economics → subjective, value-based "what SHOULD BE" statements. Depend on personal values — no data can settle them.
Positive questions are easier because they're falsifiable — you can check against evidence.
Q4Are the following statements "positive" or "normative"?
  • a) Normative — "should not be required" = value judgment
  • b) Positive — "will pay lower wages" = testable prediction
  • c) Positive — "will rise" = testable prediction
  • d) Normative — "should therefore be avoided" = value judgment
  • e) Positive — "would probably fall" = testable forecast
Q5Is each statement microeconomics or macroeconomics?
  • a) Macroeconomics — national unemployment rate
  • b) Microeconomics — wage at a specific firm
  • c) Macroeconomics — productivity of all workers
  • d) Microeconomics — wages for a specific occupation
  • e) Microeconomics — layoffs at a single plant
Q6Briefly compare the "old" and "new" labour economics.
  • Old labour economics: descriptive — focused on history of labour movements, laws, and union structures.
  • New labour economics: analytical — applies microeconomic theory to predict the behaviour of labour market participants.
📗 Tutorial 2 — Labour Market Mechanics
Q1Explain the kinds of choices confronting workers and employers in labor markets.
Workers choose:
  • Whether to enter the labour force
  • How much education/training to get
  • Which occupation/industry to enter
  • How many hours to work
  • Whether to join a union
Employers choose:
  • How many workers to hire
  • What wage and benefits to pay
  • What hours to require
  • When to lay off workers
  • Whether to outsource work
Q2Why must supply and demand be modified when applied to labor markets?
Because labour services are rented, not owned. Labour is inseparable from the worker, so:
  • Non-wage factors (safety, fairness, environment) matter as much as price
  • Markets face institutional, legal, and sociological constraints not present in commodity markets
Q3What is the relative importance of labour as an economic resource?
Labour is the most important economic resource quantitatively. In most economies, the vast majority of national income is paid out as wages and salaries — making it the primary income source for most households.
Q4What are the major features/assumptions of the economic perspective?
Three core assumptions:
  • Relative Scarcity — resources (time, income) are limited, forcing choices
  • Purposeful Behavior — rational choices by comparing costs and benefits (opportunity cost)
  • Adaptability — people respond to changes in incentives
Q5Adult population = 210M. Employed = 130M. Unemployed = 5M. Calculate unemployment rate and LFPR.
Labour Force = 130M + 5M = 135 million
Unemployment Rate = (5 / 135) × 100 = 3.7%
LFPR = (135 / 210) × 100 = 64.3%
Q6Adult pop = 9,823,000. Not in LF = 3,340,000. Employed = 6,094,000. Find LFPR and unemployment rate.
Labour Force = 9,823,000 − 3,340,000 = 6,483,000
Unemployed = 6,483,000 − 6,094,000 = 389,000
LFPR = (6,483,000 / 9,823,000) × 100 = 66.0%
Unemployment Rate = (389,000 / 6,483,000) × 100 = 6.0%
Q7Cashiers supply/demand table — equilibrium wage, employment, and effect of demand increase of 30.
  • a) Plot: Demand slopes DOWN; Supply slopes UP
  • b) Equilibrium: Wage = $6.00, Employment = 150 (where Qd = Qs)
  • c) After demand shifts right by 30 at every wage: New equilibrium = $7.00, employment = 160
📙 Tutorial Sheet 6 — Macroeconomics (IS-LM)
Q1Given C=500+0.6Yd, I=110, T=5+0.1Y, G=250. Find Y, Yd, C, savings, and multiplier.
Y = 1,863.04 billion
Yd (Disposable Income) = 1,671.74 billion
C (Consumption) = 1,503.04 billion
Private Savings = 168.7B | Public Savings = −58.7B | Investment = 110B
Multiplier = 2.17

The multiplier magnifies the effect of an initial change in autonomous spending on equilibrium output.

Q2Derive equilibrium Y and the multiplier with general behavioural equations C=c₀+c₁Yd, T=t₀+t₁Y.
Y = [c₀ − c₁t₀ + I + G] / [1 − c₁(1 − t₁)]
Multiplier = 1 / [1 − c₁(1 − t₁)]
Q3Keynesian Cross: C=300+0.5Yd, T=400, I=200, G=1000. Find Y. Then increase G and explain multiplier.
a) Y = 2,600
c) New Y (after G increase) = 2,800

The increase in output exceeds the initial ΔG because of the multiplier effect — the initial spending becomes income for others, who spend a fraction of it, creating a chain of further spending rounds.

Q4Keynesian Cross effects: What happens when G increases? When T increases? When both increase equally?
  • a) ↑G → expenditure line shifts UP → equilibrium Y increases by Multiplier × ΔG
  • b) ↑T → expenditure line shifts DOWN → equilibrium Y decreases by Multiplier × MPC × ΔT
  • c) Equal ↑G and ↑T → equilibrium Y increases by exactly ΔG (Balanced Budget Multiplier = 1)
Q5How are the IS and LM curves derived? What does their intersection mean?
  • IS Curve → derived from goods market equilibrium (Investment = Saving). Shows (i, Y) combinations. Downward sloping.
  • LM Curve → derived from financial market equilibrium (Money Demand = Money Supply). Shows (i, Y) combinations. Upward sloping.
  • Intersection = simultaneous equilibrium in BOTH goods market and financial market.
Q6IS-LM: C=400+0.25Yd, I=300+0.25Y−1500i, G=600, T=400, Md=2Y−12000i, Ms=3000. Find IS, LM, and equilibrium.
IS Curve: Y = 2400 − 3000i
LM Curve: Y = 1500 + 6000i
Equilibrium: i = 0.10 (10%), Y = 2,100
Q7In the IS-LM model: what happens with fiscal expansion (↑G)? What about monetary expansion (↑Ms)?
  • a) Fiscal Expansion ↑G → IS curve shifts RIGHT → higher interest rate AND higher output (Y)
  • b) Monetary Expansion ↑Ms → LM curve shifts RIGHT/DOWN → lower interest rate AND higher output (Y)
Quick Memory Aid

Fiscal policy → moves IS → same direction on both i and Y
Monetary policy → moves LM → opposite directions on i and Y
